# Lecture 02 — History (flashcards)

How did early computational linguistics primarily treat language?
?
As **a symbolic system governed by rules** — handcrafted grammars, dictionaries, and transformation rules.

What was a major limitation of early rule-based NLP systems?
?
They **failed outside restricted domains** — constructing comprehensive rule sets required significant human effort, and small deviations broke the system.

Which historical project aimed to remove ambiguity through symbolic design?
?
**Ars Magna** (Ramón Llull, 13th century). Wilkins's Philosophical Language and Leibniz's Characteristica Universalis are also in this lineage.

What did early symbolic NLP systems assume about meaning?
?
That meaning could be **fully formalized** — captured by a complete set of syntactic and semantic rules.

Why is studying the history of NLP valuable?
?
Because it **reveals persistent conceptual challenges** — the same questions (ambiguity, understanding, evaluation) remain open even though the tools have changed.

What was ELIZA and what year did it appear?
?
A 1966 conversational program by Joseph Weizenbaum that simulated a **Rogerian psychotherapist**.

How did ELIZA generate responses?
?
By **keyword matching and substitution** — detecting specific words and applying predefined transformation rules. No memory, no representation of meaning.

What is the ELIZA effect?
?
The **overestimation of system understanding** — humans' tendency to attribute intelligence and intention to a system based on fluent linguistic behaviour alone.

What did ELIZA demonstrate?
?
That **simple mechanisms can appear meaningful** in conversation — surface-level pattern matching can create a strong illusion of understanding.

What is the main lesson from ELIZA?
?
**Behaviour can mask lack of understanding.** Conversational success does not imply comprehension.

What does Turing's imitation test evaluate?
?
Intelligence through **observable behaviour** — whether a machine's linguistic output is indistinguishable from a human's.

What is a key limitation of Turing's approach?
?
It **may reward imitation over understanding** — a system that exploits conversational conventions can pass without comprehending.

Which modern concern echoes the ELIZA–Turing discussion about apparent understanding?
?
**Hallucinated responses** in LLMs — fluent, confident output that is nonetheless ungrounded.

A widely accepted view about current NLP systems is…?
?
…they **can perform language tasks effectively without understanding**.

What does behavioural success in NLP systems guarantee?
?
Nothing definitive — it **can be misleading** (does not guarantee semantic understanding or eliminate ambiguity).

List the chatbot milestones from 1950 to 2017.
?
1950 Turing's Test → 1966 ELIZA → 1972 PARRY → 1995 ALICE → 2011 WATSON → 2017 Self-Attention.

What does the chatbots-milestones slide note about all of these systems?
?
None of them solves the main issue of **true understanding** — they each reframe how language is modelled, but no approach guarantees comprehension.

What conceptual shift did Markov models introduce in early NLP?
?
Treating language as **observable sequences** of symbols rather than as structures endowed with semantic content — generation without grammar or meaning.
