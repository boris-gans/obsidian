# Lecture 09 — Naïve Bayes (flashcards)

What kind of model is Naïve Bayes?
?
A **probabilistic, generative** classifier based on Bayes' formula — models how documents are generated by each class rather than directly learning a decision boundary.

State the Naïve Bayes classification rule (on the formula sheet).
?
$P(y | x_1, \ldots, x_n) \propto P(y) \prod_i P(x_i | y)$ — posterior proportional to prior times product of per-feature likelihoods.

What is the central assumption of Naïve Bayes?
?
**Features are conditionally independent given the class label** — the "naïve" simplification that makes the joint likelihood factorize.

Is the Naïve Bayes assumption true in natural language?
?
No — words depend strongly on neighbours. But the classifier often works well anyway: a famous mismatch between modelling assumption and empirical performance.

Write Bayes' formula.
?
$P(A|B) = \dfrac{P(B|A)\,P(A)}{P(B)}$ — posterior equals likelihood times prior divided by evidence.

What's the difference between a generative and a discriminative classifier?
?
**Generative** models the joint $P(x,y) = P(x|y)P(y)$ — how data and labels are jointly generated. **Discriminative** models $P(y|x)$ directly.

Which is generative: Naïve Bayes or logistic regression?
?
**Naïve Bayes** is generative; **logistic regression** is discriminative.

Does logistic regression assume word independence?
?
**No** — that's a Naïve Bayes assumption. LR makes no independence claim about features (Quiz II.M2 Q18 false-statement target).

Why use log-probabilities in Naïve Bayes?
?
To **avoid numerical underflow** when multiplying many small probabilities — logs turn products into sums and stabilize computation.

What is Laplace smoothing and why is it needed?
?
Add a small constant to each count when estimating $P(x_i|y)$ to prevent **zero probabilities** — without it, one unseen word zeros out the entire posterior.

What's the only hyperparameter of basic Naïve Bayes?
?
The **smoothing constant** $\alpha$ in Laplace smoothing.

What are the four cells of a confusion matrix?
?
**TP** (True Positive), **FP** (False Positive), **TN** (True Negative), **FN** (False Negative).

Write the precision formula.
?
$\text{Precision} = \dfrac{TP}{TP + FP}$ — reliability of positive predictions.

Write the recall formula.
?
$\text{Recall} = \dfrac{TP}{TP + FN}$ — proportion of true positives recovered.

Write the F1 formula.
?
$F_1 = \dfrac{2PR}{P+R}$ — harmonic mean of precision and recall.

If a classifier predicts 100 entities and 80 are correct, what is precision?
?
$\text{Precision} = 80/100 = 0.80$.

When is accuracy misleading?
?
On **imbalanced datasets** — a classifier that always predicts the majority class can get high accuracy with near-zero recall on the minority.

What is the canonical pattern of the "accuracy trap"?
?
**High accuracy + near-zero recall** — high overall correctness driven entirely by the majority class.

When is AUC preferable to accuracy?
?
When **decision thresholds may vary** — AUC measures ranking quality across all thresholds, whereas accuracy is tied to one.

What does lowering the decision threshold typically do to recall and precision?
?
**Increase recall, decrease precision** — predicting positive more often catches more positives but raises the false-positive rate.

What are the strengths of Naïve Bayes?
?
**Fast, simple, robust**; works well with small datasets; strong baseline for text classification; interpretable (which words push toward which class).

What are the limitations of Naïve Bayes?
?
The independence assumption **ignores word order and syntax**; doesn't capture semantic meaning; saturates on more complex tasks.

Which scikit-learn variant of Naïve Bayes is standard for text classification with word counts?
?
`MultinomialNB` — assumes a multinomial distribution over word counts.
