# Cluster 5: Word meaning in vector space

> **The story of this cluster in one sentence.** Cluster 4's classifiers couldn't tell that "car" and "automobile" mean almost the same thing — this cluster fixes that by giving every word **coordinates in a low-dim space** where geometric closeness *is* semantic closeness.

## Why this cluster exists

Every classical method so far — BoW, TF-IDF, NB, LR — treats words as **orthogonal feature dimensions**. The cosine similarity between one-hot "car" and one-hot "automobile" is exactly 0 (Cluster 3's vocabulary-mismatch problem). This cluster's conceptual move is to **stop using `|V|`-dim sparse one-hot axes** and start using **`d`-dim dense learned vectors** (typically `d ≈ 100–300`), placed so that words that appear in similar contexts end up near each other. This is the deepest representational shift in the course; everything from Cluster 6 onward rides on top of it (RNNs ingest embeddings, transformers' input layer is an embedding matrix).

The blueprint flags this as **high weight**: mock Q6 (Word2Vec), Q7 (LSA), Q23 / Q25 (one-hot vs. dense), Quiz III Q3–Q9, Q17–Q18 (and Model B mirrors). Embedding-matrix sizing (`V × d`) is a numerical MCQ (Quiz III Q6, Quiz IV Q13). The formula sheet gives `xw = E[w]` and `cos(xw, xw')` — *what's not on the sheet is everything about why and how*.

## The arc

### 1. [[distributional-hypothesis]] — the philosophical claim that justifies everything

> "You shall know a word by the company it keeps." — Firth, 1957

The **distributional hypothesis** says that **words occurring in similar contexts have similar meanings**. This is a testable empirical claim, not a definition — and the rest of the cluster is methods that *operationalize it*: count contexts (HAL), factorize a context matrix (LSA), or learn vectors that predict contexts (Word2Vec). Without this claim, none of the work that follows makes sense; with it, every method is some version of "give similar-context words similar coordinates."

### 2. [[hyperspace-analogue-to-language]] — count-based context, no learning

HAL is the most literal implementation of the distributional hypothesis: for each word `w`, count how often each other word appears within a window around `w`; the resulting `|V|`-dim vector is `w`'s representation. Words with similar context-count profiles end up with similar HAL vectors. Two problems jump out: (a) vectors are still `|V|`-dim and sparse, (b) raw counts are dominated by frequent function words. The fixes — **dimensionality reduction** and **count reweighting** — motivate LSA, and eventually motivate the move to learning vectors directly. HAL is the *honest first attempt*; everything after improves on it.

### 3. [[latent-semantic-analysis]] — SVD-factorize the term-document matrix

LSA takes the term-document matrix `X` (Cluster 2's object), applies a **truncated SVD** `X ≈ Uₖ Σₖ Vₖᵀ`, and uses the rows of `Uₖ` as `k`-dim word embeddings. The geometric trick: the top-`k` singular vectors capture the directions of largest co-occurrence variance — the *latent semantic axes*. Synonymy emerges naturally because synonyms are pulled toward the same low-dim direction (the answer to vocabulary mismatch from Cluster 3). LSA is on the formula sheet (`X = UΣVᵀ`, low-rank `Xₖ = Uₖ Σₖ Vₖᵀ`); the exam asks for *interpretation* (mock Q7; Quiz III Q3–Q4). Conceptually: LSA is "compressed HAL."

### 4. [[lda]] — the probabilistic, generative cousin of LSA

Where LSA decomposes the term-document matrix with linear algebra, **Latent Dirichlet Allocation** decomposes the same information with **Bayesian inference**. A document is generated by (a) drawing a topic-mixture `θ_d ~ Dir(α)`, then (b) for each word position, picking a topic `z ~ θ_d` and a word `w ~ φ_z`. Two payoffs over LSA: topics are **probability distributions over words** (directly readable as "topic 4 = {gene, protein, …}"), and the **Dirichlet prior `α`** controls topic-mixture sparsity (the conceptual MCQ trap — small α → documents concentrate on few topics; large α → spread thin). Mock Q8, Quiz III Q5 / Q17 (and B mirrors) test the generative story and the meaning of `α`. The full Dirichlet-prior + posterior-counts machinery is on the formula sheet — what the exam tests is *what the symbols mean*.

### 5. [[word-embeddings]] — the umbrella concept

A **word embedding** is a dense, low-dim vector representation of a word, typically `d = 100–300`. The crucial property: **vectors of similar-meaning words are close in cosine distance**. This concept ties together LSA, Word2Vec, GloVe, and the embedding lookup that lives at the input layer of every later neural model. The exam tests the contrast with one-hot (mock Q23, Q25) and the size of the parameter table (next concept). Hold the umbrella in your head; the next concepts are specific implementations.

### 6. [[embedding-matrix]] — `V × d` parameters that *are* the embedding

The embedding "matrix" `E ∈ ℝ^(V × d)` is a learned table where row `i` is the dense vector for word `i`. Lookup is just indexing: `E[i]` returns a `d`-dim vector. The whole table has `V · d` parameters — for `V = 50000`, `d = 300`, that's 15 M parameters. This is what the formula sheet means by `xw = E[w]`. Quiz III Q6 / Quiz IV Q13 ask for the parameter count given `V` and `d`; the answer is *just multiplication*. The same matrix appears unchanged at the input of every transformer in Cluster 8 — embedding matrix isn't an old-NLP-only concept.

### 7. [[word2vec]] — predict-the-context, learn the vectors

Word2Vec (Mikolov 2013) gives up matrix factorization and instead **trains a shallow neural network to predict context words from a center word** (skip-gram) or vice versa (CBOW). The network has *one hidden layer*; **the hidden layer is the embedding**. After training, the input-to-hidden weight matrix `W ∈ ℝ^(V × d)` is the embedding matrix. The conceptual jump: stop counting, start *predicting* — and the gradients of the prediction loss sculpt vectors into a geometry where similar-context words are close. *Meaning becomes geometry.* Surprising consequence: linear analogies emerge (`king - man + woman ≈ queen`).

### 8. [[skip-gram-and-cbow]] — two symmetric formulations

**Skip-gram**: given the center word, predict each context word. Slow per word but learns better representations of rare words. **CBOW**: given the bag of context words, predict the center. Faster but smooths over rare words. Skip-gram is the default for NLP because rare-word quality matters. Both share the same embedding matrix `W`. The exam tests *which is which* — given a description, match it to one of the two (mock Q6, Quiz III Q7–Q9).

### 9. [[negative-sampling]] — the trick that makes Word2Vec scale

The skip-gram softmax over `|V|` words is `O(|V|)` per gradient step — prohibitively slow for `|V| = 100K+`. **Negative sampling** replaces it with a **binary classification objective**: distinguish a true (center, context) pair from `k` randomly sampled "negative" pairs (drawn from a noise distribution over the vocabulary). For each positive example, you only need to update `k + 1` ≈ 5–10 word vectors instead of all `|V|`. This is the engineering trick that made Word2Vec practical. Quiz III Q7 / Q18 ask for the *purpose* (avoid full softmax) and the *mechanism* (sample non-context words and treat them as negatives).

### 10. [[pointwise-mutual-information]] — the structure both LSA and Word2Vec recover

PMI measures how much more often two events co-occur than independence would predict: `PMI(w, c) = log P(w, c) / (P(w) P(c))`. Positive when associated, zero at independence, negative when anti-associated. The exam doesn't test PMI directly — but it's the **mathematical structure that ties this whole cluster together**. Levy & Goldberg (2014) proved that **skip-gram with negative sampling implicitly factorizes a *shifted* PMI matrix** (`v_w · v_c ≈ PMI(w, c) − log k`). The classical `PPMI + SVD` recipe (positive PMI, then truncated SVD) is essentially LSA's count-based cousin. Conclusion: **count-based and predict-based embeddings are not rival paradigms — they're two algorithmic paths to the same factorization.** Hold this; it dissolves the LSA-vs-Word2Vec dichotomy.

## Connections worth seeing

- **LSA and Word2Vec compute (almost) the same thing two different ways.** LSA *explicitly* factorizes a co-occurrence matrix via SVD; skip-gram-with-negative-sampling *implicitly* factorizes a shifted PMI matrix via gradient descent. The discovery (Levy & Goldberg, 2014) is that count-based and predict-based methods are not rivals — they're two algorithmic paths to the same factorization.
- **The embedding matrix is the "input layer" of every neural model in the rest of the course.** RNN inputs (Cluster 7), transformer inputs (Cluster 8), HuggingFace token inputs (Cluster 9) all start with `E[token_id]`. This `V × d` table is the same object across every architecture; only the layers *after* it change.
- **Negative sampling is structurally identical to the binary cross-entropy of Cluster 4's logistic regression.** "Is this (word, context) pair real (1) or noise (0)?" is a binary classification task with a sigmoid output and BCE loss — Word2Vec is, under the hood, a giant batch of LR problems sharing parameters.
- **Cosine similarity of embeddings (here) is the same metric as cosine similarity of TF-IDF vectors (Cluster 3) — but the vector axes are wholly different.** TF-IDF axes = words (sparse, `|V|`-dim); embedding axes = latent semantic dimensions (dense, `d`-dim). The same operation, in two qualitatively different spaces.
- **Distributional hypothesis (here) → contextual embeddings (Cluster 8).** Word2Vec gives one vector per word, averaging all senses ("bank" gets a single point between "river" and "money"). Transformers fix this by computing a **per-context** vector at every position via self-attention. Same hypothesis, finer grain.

## Common confusions

- **Word2Vec is a "shallow neural network."** One hidden layer. Don't call it deep.
- **Skip-gram vs. CBOW** — skip-gram predicts context from center; CBOW predicts center from context. Skip-gram is preferred for rare words.
- **LSA vs. Word2Vec** — LSA factorizes a count matrix explicitly (SVD); Word2Vec factorizes a PMI matrix implicitly (SGD). Both produce dense word vectors.
- **LSA vs. LDA** — both decompose the term-document matrix into latent structure. LSA is *deterministic and linear* (SVD); LDA is *probabilistic and generative* (Dirichlet-prior topic mixtures). LDA topics are interpretable as word distributions; LSA dimensions are abstract directions.
- **Dirichlet `α` direction** — small α → sparse (documents focus on few topics); large α → dense (documents spread across all topics). Easy to flip under exam pressure.
- **Embedding matrix shape** — `V × d` (rows = vocab words, cols = embedding dimensions). Confusing this trips up the parameter-count MCQ.
- **One-hot vs. dense embedding** — one-hot is `V`-dim sparse and pairwise orthogonal; embedding is `d`-dim dense and *captures similarity*. Mock Q23, Q25 are exactly this contrast.
- **Word2Vec captures polysemy?** No — one vector per word, averaging senses. This is the *gap that motivates contextual embeddings* in Cluster 8.

## Self-check (synthesis, not recall)

1. **Negative sampling and binary cross-entropy (Cluster 4) are doing the same thing.** Spell out the correspondence: what's the "label" in negative sampling, what's the "score," what's the loss? Why does it scale better than the full softmax?
2. **Word2Vec famously satisfies `king - man + woman ≈ queen`.** Why does this *linear arithmetic* work — what does it imply about how gender and royalty are represented in the embedding space? What kind of relation would *not* be captured this way?
3. **You have 1 GB of news articles and want word embeddings.** Compare LSA (truncated SVD) and Word2Vec (skip-gram + negative sampling) on (a) training cost, (b) ability to handle very rare words, (c) ability to update with new data. Which would you pick and why?
4. **The vocabulary-mismatch problem** (Cluster 3) was that "car" and "automobile" had cosine similarity 0 in TF-IDF. After this cluster's methods, what is the cosine similarity of their embeddings? What would *break* if the corpus you trained on never used both words?
5. **Word2Vec gives "bank" one vector.** What sentence pairs would you construct to demonstrate this is a problem? What architectural change in Cluster 8 fixes it?
6. **Looking forward to Cluster 6:** word embeddings give you a vector *per word*. But many NLP tasks need a *label per word* — POS tags, named-entity types — and the right tag depends on what came *before* in the sentence. What kind of model would you reach for to make per-token predictions that condition on sequence position?
